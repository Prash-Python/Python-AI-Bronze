To build an effective KNN model for loan default, we have to handle two critical things that the previous linear model didn't require: Feature Scaling (because KNN relies on distances) and Categorical Encoding (to turn "Salaried" into numbers).

1. The KNN Classification LogicThe K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) algorithm predicts a customer's risk by looking at the $k$ most similar past customers in the "feature space." If most similar customers defaulted, the model predicts a default for the new applicant.

Financial Risk InterpretationWhy is Scaling Mandatory?In KNN, distance is calculated using the Euclidean formula:$$d(p, q) = \sqrt{\sum (p_i - q_i)^2}$$If we don't scale, a change of 50 points in CreditScore will completely overwhelm a change of 5 years in LoanTerm, even though the loan term might be a more important risk factor.Class Imbalance AwarenessIn real banking data, 95% of people pay back loans, and only 5% default.The Trap: A model could simply guess "No Default" for everyone and be 95% accurate, while being totally useless at catching risky customers.The Solution: Use Precision and Recall instead of Accuracy. In banking, we prioritize Recall (catching every possible defaulter, even if we accidentally flag a few safe ones).How to Improve Accuracy?Find the Optimal K: Use a "Scree Plot" or Elbow Method to test different values of $k$ (e.g., 1 to 15) to see which yields the lowest error rate.Feature Engineering: Create a Debt-to-Income Ratio feature ($LoanAmount / AnnualIncome$). This single number is often more predictive than the two values separately.
